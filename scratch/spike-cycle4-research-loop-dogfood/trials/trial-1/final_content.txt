## Research Log — Bounded Iteration 1

**Question:** What conditions make pre-specifiable routing (class-c decomposition) reliable at the cheap-orchestrator tier vs. when does it degrade?

**Findings:**
- Reliability at the cheap-orchestrator tier is contingent on **low-latency execution paths**, **efficient protocol overhead**, and **stable infrastructure conditions** — the routing decision must be precomputed and cheap to dispatch without triggering adaptive re-allocation.
- Degradation emerges under **high traffic load** or **network instability**, where the pre-specified decomposition no longer maps cleanly onto available compute, forcing the orchestrator into reactive fallback that violates the pre-specification contract.
- A third axis: **class-c decomposition fidelity** — the more fine-grained the routing tree, the more brittle it becomes under resource contention; coarse-grained class-C is more robust but less useful for targeted dispatch.

**Tensions:**
- **Flexibility vs. performance:** Pre-specifying routing trades runtime adaptability for predictability; at the cheap tier this tradeoff is sharper because fallback capacity is thin.
- **Decomposition granularity vs. robustness:** Finer class-C splits improve targeting precision but multiply failure points under contention; coarser splits survive instability but sacrifice the routing benefit.
- **Computational budget as balancer:** Whether increased compute at the cheap tier resolves or merely shifts these tensions remains an open empirical question — the ensemble flagged this as unresolved.
