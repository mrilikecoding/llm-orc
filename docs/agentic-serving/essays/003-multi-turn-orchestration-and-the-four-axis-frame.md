# Multi-Turn Orchestration and the Four-Axis Frame

*2026-04-30*

## Abstract

This essay reports findings from Cycle 2 of the agentic-serving scoped corpus, spanning two literature reviews and three empirical spikes on the cycle's actual deployment. Loop 1 covered shape inventory, failure modes, mitigations, and benefits-side findings (28 cited sources). Loop 4 covered ensemble design principles after the early spikes opened that gap (additional sources covering MARG-style concatenation, heterogeneity findings, refinement-loop limits, and Jiang et al.'s April 2026 single-agent information-theoretic argument). Spike B refuted small-model substitution as a latency optimization on the existing production code-review ensemble — cascade plumbing dominates wall-clock regardless of reviewer-tier compute. Spike A showed that the same existing ensemble's two-stage summarization design is dominated on every measured axis except dimension coverage by a strong prompt-steered single cloud orchestrator with no cascade (A2 ~19.5s, 16 specific recommendations, vs A1 71–145s, 9 recommendations collapsed to table form). Spike A3 — a novel ensemble *designed against* Loop 4's findings (script-based deterministic analyzer + two heterogeneous cloud LLM reviewers from different families + MARG-style concatenation aggregator with no synthesizer collapse) — produced a moderate pass: A3 surfaced what A2 structurally cannot (verified link counts, confirmed section presence, undefined model-profile bugs in the README's YAML examples that A2 missed across three trials), and the heterogeneity-uncorrelated-errors mechanism was directly observable (the two reviewers produced 5–8 distinct recommendations each with 1–2 overlap), at a latency cost of ~81s median (4× A2 but bounded under A1's 145s). A scoping caveat on the recommendation count: ~22 recommendations across three sections is the structural total, but R1-Hunyuan's chain-of-thought leakage means roughly half of those (R1's ~7–8) are accessible only after extraction work from ~18,000 characters of reasoning narration. The directly-consumable count on first read is closer to ~10 (R2-Kimi's ~6–8 cleanly numbered + the script's 3 factual sections); R1's content-equivalent specificity is real but format-degraded. The essay's framing realignment placed the cycle's design priorities (performance, environmental cost, local-first preference, token cost — priorities, not measurable optimization axes) above the performance-only optimization most published work assumes. ADR-011's single-Model-Profile commitment is defensible *as a default* — and as the right choice for tasks fitting Anthropic's threshold conditions — but is *not* a ceiling for task classes where factual grounding via script-based agent slots adds capability prompt-steering structurally cannot replicate. The cycle's premise that well-architected processes can achieve good results is partially supported empirically: the script-agent slot earns its place where deterministic verification is part of the task, MARG-style concatenation preserves what cascade-with-collapse-summarization destroys, and heterogeneous reviewer models produce uncorrelated findings on the cycle's task class as the literature predicted. Ensembles-of-ensembles, the practitioner-named affordance not tested in this cycle, is now empirically motivated as a follow-up.

## The Starting State

The agentic-serving scoped corpus entered its second cycle with two prior essays in place and a substantial architectural surface already deployed. Essay 001 produced the four-layer architecture (serving, runtime, ensemble engine, knowledge graph), the closed five-tool orchestrator surface (`invoke_ensemble`, `compose_ensemble`, `list_ensembles`, `query_knowledge`, `record_outcome` per ADR-003), the Calibration Gate for composed ensembles (ADR-007), and decisions about result summarization, autonomy gating, and turn-boundary delegation. Essay 002 ran a backward research loop from PLAY and validated two deployment shapes for single-ask tasks: a local-only configuration with qwen3:8b plus a biased system prompt, and a hybrid configuration with a cloud orchestrator (MiniMax M2.5 Free via OpenCode Zen) over local ensembles. Essay 002's central finding for compositional shapes was that prompt steering of a capable single orchestrator outperformed structural composition (CAP-2's router-executor pattern, ~2.5× latency overhead) on the examined task class; supporting literature cited (LangGraph supervisor ~30% overhead; AutoGen GroupChat one full LLM call per agent turn) was directionally consistent.

The scope of essay 002's compositional finding was narrow: qwen3:8b tier × single-ask × capability-query task class. Essay 002 explicitly named multi-turn sustained work as the most important unanswered question for the project's user contract, and named composition as structurally live but never empirically entered. Cycle 2 takes up both at once: what does sustained multi-turn agentic work demand that single-ask trials did not exercise, and what compositional shapes are worth reaching for under a capable orchestrator?

The cycle's research-entry moment applied constraint-removal per ADR-082 (an RDD methodology ADR; the project's own decision corpus contains ADRs 001–011). The most consequential existing artifact bracketed was ADR-011 (the orchestrator-LLM-is-a-Model-Profile commitment), and the practitioner's response to the constraint-removal prompt explicitly held ADR-011 as a working assumption to be tested against the literature rather than as a foundational premise. The practitioner also surfaced two priors that shaped the question set: first, a recall that prior research has reported multi-agent structures reinforcing bias and hallucination, with an open question about whether documented mitigations would allow benefits to outweigh drawbacks; second, an observation that the hybrid CAP-9 deployment pattern (cloud orchestrator + local ensembles) is the load-bearing enabler for any multi-orchestrator territory on consumer hardware, because local-only deployments make multi-orchestrator latency arithmetic immediately unworkable.

The research-methods reviewer flagged the question set as conditionally ready, with one consequential P1 missing question (a prompt-steering baseline for compositional shapes) that was adopted as RQ-2a, and a P2 benefits-side gap in RQ-3 that was adopted as RQ-3c. The five questions plus three sub-questions entered Step 1.5 with the methods reviewer's sign-off.

## The Design Priorities the Cycle Is Actually Navigating

The cycle's compositional-shapes question is not navigating a single optimization axis. The practitioner's interest spans four design priorities. These are *priorities*, not measurable axes of an optimization function — environmental cost and local-first preference do not have units, measurement methods, or thresholds in this essay, and treating them as commensurable coordinates would imply a Pareto analysis the essay does not perform. They are named here as the design values that inform configuration choice across deployments.

The four-priorities frame was adopted at the Loop 1 lit-review synthesis exchange, in response to practitioner pushback on an initial agent synthesis that overweighted performance findings and underweighted the practitioner's named optimization scope. Recording this honestly: the frame is one valid choice among alternatives, not the only defensible reading of the literature. A performance-only frame would reach similar configuration recommendations on the spikes that ran (A2 wins on performance, environmental cost, and token cost simultaneously on Spike A; A3's added value is shape-conditional rather than priority-conditional), but would close the novel-ensemble research program faster than the four-priorities frame did. The four-priorities frame's directional work — preserving the next-cycle territory rather than closing it — is what justifies its adoption here, with the caveat that "we adopted this frame because it serves the cycle's research program" is a different claim than "this frame is the most evidence-supported reading of the literature."

- **Performance** — does the configuration produce useful agentic work at acceptable quality on representative task classes?
- **Environmental cost** — what is the energy footprint of the configuration's compute, both per-session and amortized over deployment lifetime?
- **Local-first preference** — to what degree does the configuration preserve user control over what runs on owned hardware versus what is shipped to remote inference?
- **Token cost** — what is the recurring financial cost of cloud inference, both at session boundaries and across sustained workflows?

These priorities interact directionally without being commensurable. Frontier-tier cloud orchestration prioritizes performance and unfavorably weights the other three; local-only small-model deployment deprioritizes performance in favor of the other three. The hybrid pattern essay 002 validated (cloud orchestrator + local ensembles) sits between: cheap-fast cloud orchestrator latency at the cost of some token cost and a non-zero portion of compute leaving owned hardware, paired with local ensemble execution that keeps the bulk of work environmentally and locally accountable.

The framing matters because most published work on multi-agent orchestration optimizes for the first axis alone, on frontier-tier cloud-billed inference. Anthropic's multi-agent research system, the MASS framework, the Mixture-of-Agents work, and the LangGraph / AutoGen / CrewAI engineering benchmarks are all measured on GPT-class or Gemini-class or Claude-class models. The findings are valid for what they measured; they are not directly transferable to the configuration space the cycle cares about, which is small-model parallel specialists on local hardware under a cheap (cloud or local) orchestrator.

This is the essay's central reframe: the literature's negative findings on multi-agent territory are mostly findings about the configurations that exist in the literature, not findings about the configuration space the cycle wants to investigate. Where the literature reaches the cycle's territory directly (Rahman & Schranz on LLM-mediated swarm coordination; the OPTIMA result on Llama 3 8B with fine-tuning), the findings are more constraint-conditional than refutational. The territory is mostly unmeasured.

The research loop ran a single combined lit-reviewer dispatch covering all five RQs and three sub-questions. The dispatch included the cycle's four design priorities as framing context, the constraint-removal exchange (so ADR-011 was framed as a working assumption to be tested), and explicit scope conditions on capability tier and deployment shape. Citation provenance and argument coherence are audited downstream of this essay per Step 4a–4b.

Two scope conditions on the lit-review's findings deserve explicit naming. First, the literature on multi-turn agentic dynamics post-dates most of the cycle's prior research, with several primary references published in the first quarter of 2026 (the HORIZON benchmark, AMA-Bench, LongCLI-Bench, the Khanal et al. reliability framework). The findings are recent, recently-peer-reviewed where applicable, and have not had time to accumulate replication studies. Second, the lit-reviewer's coverage of bio-inspired collective-intelligence patterns in LLM architecture terms (eusocial, ant-colony, naked-mole-rat) was honest about absence: the literature in those specific terms is sparse, and the closest published analogues are stigmergy (shared-memory-trace coordination, operationalized in the SwarmSys system) and ACO (a classical algorithm whose LLM-mediated implementations are slow). The cycle does not synthesize what the literature did not produce.

## What the Literature Settled

The findings that follow are scoped to the specific evidence the lit-review surfaced. Where the evidence is at frontier tier, the synthesis names the gap to the cycle's qwen3:8b operating tier rather than extrapolating across it.

### Long-horizon performance degrades super-linearly

The HORIZON benchmark (Wang et al., arXiv:2604.11978, April 2026), tested across more than 3,100 trajectories on GPT-5 variants and Claude models, found that performance on short and mid-horizon tasks was strong while long-horizon tasks broke down — the framework's "long-horizon task mirage." Khanal et al. (arXiv:2603.29231) formalized this as four reliability metrics with a counterintuitive finding: frontier models exhibit *higher* performance variance at long horizons because ambitious multi-step plans spiral rather than degrade gracefully. Meltdown rates of up to 19 percent occur on long-horizon tasks even in frontier models.

Two specific mechanisms drive the degradation. AMA-Bench (Zhao et al., arXiv:2602.22769, ICLR 2026 Memory Agent workshop) showed that similarity-based memory retrieval loses causally necessary information even when context capacity is not the binding constraint; the best-available memory system achieves 57.22 percent accuracy on real agentic trajectories, failing more than 40 percent of the time. LongCLI-Bench (Feng et al., arXiv:2602.14337) on CLI-agentic coding tasks found state-of-the-art agents pass less than 20 percent of long-horizon tasks, with most stalls occurring at less than 30 percent completion — failures are predominantly early, not late.

For llm-orc specifically, the relevant finding from the "Lost in the Middle" literature (Liu et al., arXiv:2307.03172, 2024) is that models under-weight middle-of-context content. In a multi-turn cascade, tool-dispatch results from earlier turns sit precisely in the mid-context attention valley.

Tau-bench (Yao et al., 2024 — distinct from the 2025 meta-judge / panel-discussion paper cited later) is the most directly relevant published baseline for the multi-turn tool-dispatching regime that essay 003's spike battery does not reach: GPT-4o achieves under 50% task success on tau-bench's tool-agent-user multi-turn interaction tasks, and pass^8 (consistency across eight runs) drops below 25% even for frontier models. The cycle's three spikes were single-ask code-review tasks; they do not test the sustained multi-turn tool-dispatching regime tau-bench measures. The cycle's empirical strengthening of ADR-011 should be read as scoped to the single-ask task classes tested, not as evidence about the multi-turn regime where tau-bench's reliability ceiling is the relevant constraint. The system's per-turn schema overhead (~15K tokens of input from the closed five-tool surface plus the OpenCode-declared client tools) compounds this: by mid-session, earlier tool results are occupying mid-context positions where the orchestrator is structurally less likely to attend to them.

The implication for RQ-1 is that single-ask findings do not extrapolate cleanly to multi-turn. Failure modes characterized in essay 002 (slow-useful, fast-confabulation, fast-giveup, premature-stop, summarization-broken, unsupported, argument-confabulation) are starting hypotheses for multi-turn behavior but not endpoints. The literature names additional multi-turn-specific modes (meltdown onset, error self-conditioning, memory retrieval drift) that the single-ask taxonomy did not surface.

### Composition's threshold conditions are concrete

Anthropic's June 2025 engineering writeup on their multi-agent research system gave the clearest production-derived conditions for when composition earns its complexity: when task value justifies a 15× token overhead versus single-agent chat, when genuine parallelization opportunities exist, and when information scope exceeds single-context-window limits. The third condition is the most often binding — for tasks that fit in one context window and do not benefit from parallelism (which describes most agentic coding queries), Anthropic's own guidance is to use a single agent.

The MASS framework (Zhou et al., arXiv:2502.02533, Google / Cambridge) measured the empirical contributions of two independent levers on Gemini 1.5 Pro: prompt optimization adds approximately 6 percentage points over default single-agent chain-of-thought baseline; correct topology selection adds approximately 5 more percentage points on top. The paper's most consequential finding for the cycle is that most topology choices are neutral or harmful — only a small fraction are beneficial. Uninformed topology selection produces no improvement over single-agent.

For the cycle, the threshold is not algebraic but configuration-conditional. The compose_ensemble primitive earns its presence on tasks meeting Anthropic's three conditions (parallelizable, context-window-exceeding, cost-justifying); for tasks that do not, prompt steering of a single capable orchestrator is the cheaper sufficient intervention. The MASS finding qualifies this: even on tasks where structural composition could in principle help, the topology-selection problem is a hard sub-problem that a runtime-composition primitive must navigate or punt on.

### Prompt steering generalizes directionally as the dominant lever

Essay 002's CAP-2 finding (prompt steering > structural composition at qwen3:8b on single-ask capability queries) generalizes directionally per the literature. Anthropic's own framing of their multi-agent system explicitly named prompt steering as "the single most important way to guide agent behavior" — even within a multi-agent architecture. An engineering analysis from the Iterathon 2026 series found that on a customer-support task at frontier-model tier (GPT-5.2), a single-agent approach with better prompting achieved 92 percent of a three-agent workflow's performance at 28 percent of its cost — the scope of that finding is narrower than essay 002's task class but directionally consistent.

The comparison breaks for tasks requiring breadth-first parallel exploration across sources whose collective scope exceeds a single context window. For those tasks, structural composition provides what prompt steering cannot, because the binding constraint is information capacity rather than reasoning quality. Whether the cycle's multi-turn coding sessions approach that regime is the open empirical question RQ-2a is positioned to test. Sustained multi-turn coding work — repository exploration, multi-file refactors, debugging across modules — could plausibly exceed single-context-window capacity in ways single-ask tasks do not, but the literature does not measure this directly.

### Shape inventory: most candidates are conditional, not categorical, choices

The lit-review's shape inventory landed in five practical categories. Single orchestrator is the lowest-overhead baseline and the recommended starting point in production guidance. Hierarchical supervisor-plus-workers earns its cost for genuinely decomposable parallel work and is the architecture Anthropic's research system uses. Semantic-routed ensembles (operationalized in ZOOTER, NAACL 2024) are well-supported in the literature; llm-orc's existing `invoke_ensemble` already implements a form of this — the orchestrator selects an ensemble by name based on the user's request semantics. Mixture-of-Agents at the orchestration layer (Wang et al., arXiv:2406.04692) achieves 65.1 percent on AlpacaEval 2.0 surpassing GPT-4o, but at frontier model tier — not validated at qwen3:8b. Multi-agent debate is conditions-dependent and shares failure modes with the broader category, treated below.

Two shapes from the practitioner-named candidate set warrant explicit discussion. Swarm-of-small-models, as Rahman and Schranz (arXiv:2506.14496) measured it, is not viable for non-trivial coordination scales: the LLM-mediated coordination overhead exceeds classical algorithms by approximately 36,000× for equivalent coordination tasks. The paper's recommended hybrid is LLMs for strategic reasoning and classical algorithms for low-level coordination. The scope of this finding matters for the cycle: Rahman and Schranz measured LLMs replacing classical coordination algorithms (where the classical algorithm is the right tool for the job and the LLM substitution is gratuitous). A *parallel specialist* shape — multiple small-model workers each prompted for a specific sub-task, with no inter-agent coordination — is structurally different and is not directly tested by their measurement. Parallel specialists still incur an aggregation-overhead cost (the orchestrator must combine worker outputs into a coherent response), but the aggregation cost is bounded by the orchestrator's per-turn capacity rather than by the multiplicative coordination penalty that emerged in the LLM-mediated swarm setup.

Bio-inspired collective-intelligence patterns (eusocial, ant-colony, naked-mole-rat) in those specific terms are sparse in LLM-architecture literature. The closest published analogues are stigmergy (the SwarmSys system, arXiv:2510.10047, in which agents coordinate through shared memory traces rather than synchronous communication) and ant colony optimization (a classical algorithm; LLM-mediated implementations fall in the slow case Rahman and Schranz characterized). The cycle inherits this honestly: the practitioner-named bio-inspired candidates do not have direct LLM-architecture literature to draw on, but the closest analogues — stigmergy and parallel role-specialization — map cleanly onto local-first deployments where each worker's compute footprint is small.

### Failure modes verified, with mitigations available

The practitioner's prior recall on multi-agent bias and hallucination amplification is verified by the literature, and the failure mode is more pointed than the generic recall suggested.

Li et al. (ICLR 2026, OpenReview mo7u21GoQv) found that structured multi-agent workflows amplify minor stochastic biases into systemic polarization even when individual agents are neutral. Their most striking finding is what they call the "Trigger Vulnerability" — injecting *objective* context into a debate accelerates polarization rather than moderating it. This inverts the intuitive mitigation (provide more context to ground the agents) and is the key result for any cycle considering multi-orchestrator coordination protocols. Madigan et al. (arXiv:2512.16433) corroborated with a finding that emergent collective bias is not attributable to individual components — the bias is a property of the coordination protocol, not the agents. Wynn et al. (ICML MAS Workshop 2025, arXiv:2509.05396) showed that multi-agent debate can decrease accuracy via sycophancy, even when stronger models numerically outnumber weaker ones in a debate population.

Five mitigations are documented with varying evidence quality. Anonymization of debate identity labels (Choi et al., ACL 2026 Main, arXiv:2510.07517) is the strongest evidence base — the mitigation requires no retraining, works across model families, and has main-conference acceptance. Anti-conformity prompting paired with trajectory-based scoring (FREE-MAD, arXiv:2509.11035) reports approximately 50 percent token reduction alongside the conformity-resistance improvement. Reasoning-tree auditing with minority-correct training (AgentAuditor, arXiv:2602.09341) achieves approximately 5 points over majority-vote baseline. Heterogeneous model diversity across families is recommended as a structural mitigation across the literature, though without single-source quantitative evidence. Judge architectures and role-asymmetry are widely deployed in shipped systems as failure-mode-conditional mitigations.

None of the five mitigations fully eliminates the echo-chamber failure mode. A scope condition is load-bearing here: all five mitigations target multi-agent debate topologies specifically. The trigger-vulnerability and bias-amplification findings are also primarily measured on debate-shaped coordination protocols. llm-orc's actual coordination shape — supervisor-routing through `invoke_ensemble` plus cascading tool dispatch — is not debate. Whether the mitigation evidence translates to llm-orc's coordination shape is empirically open, and whether the trigger-vulnerability finding (objective context injection accelerates polarization) applies to llm-orc's natural grounding-context injection patterns is also open. The territory is recoverable by design but not by default; any cycle proceeding into multi-orchestrator coordination must commit to one or more mitigations as a condition of operation, not treat them as optional polish — and must acknowledge that the mitigations' applicability to non-debate coordination shapes is itself an empirical question the literature does not settle.

### Benefits side: real, scoped, mostly at frontier tier

Multi-agent structures show reproducible empirical benefits under specific conditions. Anthropic's research system reported 90.2 percent improvement on internal research evaluations at frontier model tier with a 15× token cost — the benefits are real and the costs are explicit. Tasks exceeding single-context-window scope and tasks with available domain-specialist models are the two conditions most often cited as making structural composition pay. OPTIMA on Llama 3 8B (Chen et al., ACL 2025 Findings) achieved a 2.8× performance gain at less than 10 percent of token cost relative to baseline multi-agent — but the result requires OPTIMA fine-tuning that the cycle's deployment does not have, and the comparison baseline is unoptimized multi-agent rather than single-agent.

The capability-tier finding is the most consequential for the cycle. All strong empirical evidence for multi-agent benefits uses frontier models; no published paper validates multi-agent composition benefits at the qwen3:8b tier without fine-tuning. Read through the cycle's four design priorities, this re-reads as opportunity rather than limitation — *conditional on the configuration being a priori viable for the task class at this tier*. The conditionality is load-bearing: if the configuration is not viable, the unmeasured space is unmeasured because the niche has been (implicitly) judged not worth measuring, not because the literature has overlooked it. The cycle's spikes are positioned to test viability directly. The MASS finding of approximately 5 percentage points from topology at Gemini 1.5 Pro is the closest analogue worth testing at qwen3:8b. The OPTIMA result on Llama 3 8B (2.8× performance gain at <10% tokens) is encouraging directionally but is *not* a directly testable analogue for this cycle — OPTIMA requires a fine-tuning step the cycle's deployment does not have, and the comparison baseline is unoptimized multi-agent rather than single-agent.

### The frontier shows tradeoffs more than dominance

Across the speed × performance × cost axes, no single Pareto frontier emerged from the literature because most benchmarks measure one or two axes rather than three. Point estimates sketch the shape. LangGraph's 2026 community benchmark reported approximately 5 percent token overhead with approximately 1.2-second average latency on 10-step research pipelines using GPT-4o — substantially lower than essay 002's cited 30-percent figure for LangGraph supervisor overhead, suggesting either methodology divergence or framework evolution. The implication is worth naming explicitly: essay 002 used the 30 percent figure as supporting literature for the CAP-2 structural-composition rejection. If the current figure is closer to 5 percent, that specific supporting evidence is weaker than essay 002 implied — though essay 002's empirical CAP-2 finding (the ~2.5× latency overhead measured directly in this codebase) is independent of the LangGraph figure and stands on its own. AutoGen's conversational mode generates 20+ LLM calls per task. CrewAI consumed nearly twice the tokens of LangGraph on the 2026 comparison and ran approximately 3× slower.

For local hardware specifically, community engineering analyses of Qwen3-0.6B / 1.7B / 4B inference report rates of 5–8 tokens per second on CPU and 40–60+ tokens per second on single GPU with speculative decoding (lit-review source 24, aggregating dasroot.net / cordum.io / latenode.com community benchmarks; the Qwen3 Technical Report's official benchmarks cover GPU inference on NVIDIA H20 only). At per-turn context sizes of 15,000+ tokens (the orchestrator's tool schema overhead), the community-reported CPU figures translate to time-to-first-token on the order of 300–500 seconds even for the smallest models. The local-only configuration's binding constraint is token-throughput on consumer CPUs rather than model capability — smaller models alone do not address it, because the per-turn input context is large regardless of model size. This is the structural reason the hybrid pattern (cloud orchestrator + local ensembles) closes the gap: it eliminates the orchestrator's per-turn input-context throughput bottleneck.

Read across the four design priorities, these point estimates are more permissive than a performance-only read. Frontier-tier multi-agent at 15× token cost privileges performance and degrades the other three; local small-model parallel specialists privilege environmental cost, local-first preference, and token cost while pacing performance. The hybrid pattern occupies a middle position that is interactive-acceptable on latency and modest on token cost. Operators choose configurations based on which priorities bind for their workflow.

## The Capability-Tier Gap as Empirical Opportunity

*Note: this section was written before the cycle's spikes ran; the spike findings appear in §"What the Spikes Found" below. The framing here is preserved as the essay's pre-spike orientation toward the territory.*


The most consequential synthesis from the lit-review is that the literature's multi-agent benefit evidence sits at frontier tier, while the cycle's local-first deployment sits at the qwen3:8b tier, and the gap between them is mostly unmeasured. The question for the cycle is not whether multi-agent structures are worth investigating — the literature has answers for frontier tier — but whether the published findings extend down to small-model parallel specialists on local hardware.

The MASS finding gives a concrete number to test. At Gemini 1.5 Pro, structural composition adds approximately 5 percentage points over prompt-optimized single-agent baseline on representative task classes. If that delta materializes at qwen3:8b on multi-turn coding sessions, the structural-composition surface earns its complexity in the cycle's deployment. If it washes out at smaller scale, the single-Model-Profile commitment in ADR-011 holds with stronger empirical support than essay 002 could provide alone. Either result is informative; the question is which.

A second test sits at the deployment-shape boundary. Rahman and Schranz's swarm coordination penalty is for LLM-mediated coordination tasks; a parallel-specialist shape with no inter-agent coordination is a different setup. Whether multiple Qwen3-0.6B / 1.7B / 4B workers running concurrently on consumer hardware produce a workable latency profile for a coding-session task class is empirically open. The published per-token inference rates are a starting point; the actual configuration's latency under real workloads is the measurement.

These are spike-shaped questions: focused, scratch-directory scope, clear pass/fail signals. Both anchor essay claims with cycle-specific empirical evidence rather than literature alone. The validation-spike decision per ADR-087 fires at Step 4c after this essay enters audit; the practitioner's "follow the research" posture and explicit endorsement of essay-first-then-spikes is recorded in the research log.

## The Conductor Experience Gap

The peer-reviewed HCI research on sustained agentic session experience is sparse. The lit-review honestly named this gap. The published guidance that exists comes from related but indirect work: under-4-second targets for conversational AI (arXiv:2507.22352); moderate-latency-with-context-and-progress-signals tolerance (Tandfonline 2025); the 2-second standard for chatbot response that essay 002's 6-minute local-only configuration violates by approximately 180×. None of these directly characterize agentic coding session tolerance.

The engineering practice has more concrete answers than peer-reviewed HCI does. Claude Code (Liu et al., arXiv:2604.14228, April 2026) describes seven graduated autonomy modes spanning plan to bypass-permissions, paired with append-only session persistence via JSONL. Anthropic's context-engineering work uses the term "progressive disclosure" for an agent-side context-discovery pattern (the agent incrementally pulls context as it works). The essay borrows the term by analogy for operator-side observability — the analogy is the essay's synthesis, not a direct attribution to Anthropic's framing — and under that analogy users see agent state at the level of detail they need, not full trace dumps.

The cycle's PLAY-phase finding from Cycle 1 (user passivity in the face of silent failure as the dominant experiential mode) is consistent with what engineering sources describe but lacks peer-reviewed validation. For RQ-5, the implication is that empirical investigation of the conductor experience belongs at PLAY in this cycle, not at research. The literature provides framing concepts (progressive disclosure, graduated autonomy, fork-and-rollback, confidence-based clarification) that the cycle can adopt as design hypotheses; testing whether they hold for the specific workflows the cycle's users care about is observational work, not literature work.

## Open Empirical Questions

*Note: this section was written before the cycle's spikes ran. It records the empirical questions the literature did not reach and the spike candidates' pre-run framing. The actual spike outcomes are reported in §"What the Spikes Found" below.*


The lit-review opened four genuinely empirical questions that cannot be resolved from the literature alone. Two are clean spike candidates; two are larger probes that fit BUILD or PLAY phases of the cycle better than research spikes.

The clean spike candidates are sketched as questions; both will need to be sharpened into falsifiable hypotheses before the spike battery runs.

Spike candidate A (MASS-equivalent at qwen3:8b multi-turn): does the MASS topology delta (~5 percentage points over prompt-optimized single-agent baseline at Gemini 1.5 Pro) materialize at qwen3:8b on multi-turn coding sessions? Specifying the spike requires committing to a task class (e.g., a multi-file refactor across a representative repository), a comparison baseline (prompt-optimized single qwen3:8b orchestrator), a specific topology to test (e.g., the supervisor-plus-three-specialists pattern that approximates Anthropic's research-system shape), and a threshold for "materializes" (e.g., delta of at least 3 percentage points on outcome quality with no more than 2× latency increase). The spike is a question until those parameters are committed.

Spike candidate B (parallel-specialist latency profile on consumer hardware): does a parallel-specialist shape — multiple Qwen3-0.6B / 1.7B / 4B workers running concurrently under a cheap orchestrator — produce a workable latency profile, or does even no-coordination concurrency hit an inference-throughput ceiling? Specifying the spike requires a definition of "workable" (e.g., time-to-first-orchestrator-output under 10 seconds for a representative coding query), a model count (e.g., 8 concurrent workers — production analyses suggest 8–12 as the consumer-hardware sweet spot), and an aggregation overhead measurement (the orchestrator's cost to aggregate worker outputs is non-zero even when workers do not coordinate among themselves; this is distinct from the inter-agent coordination cost Rahman and Schranz measured).

Both spike candidates, once their parameters are committed to falsifiable hypotheses, are positioned to anchor the cycle's claims in deployment-specific empirical evidence and address the two most consequential gaps the lit-review surfaced.

The larger probes are: first, whether the long-horizon error accumulation pattern characterized by HORIZON and LongCLI-Bench reproduces in llm-orc's multi-turn user-driven session shape (which differs from both single-ask and fully-autonomous long-horizon benchmarks); second, whether Li et al.'s trigger-vulnerability finding for multi-agent debate translates to llm-orc's actual coordination protocol (supervisor-routing plus cascading tool dispatch, which is not debate). Both of these benefit from being exercised in actual coding workflows rather than in scratch-directory probes — they fit BUILD or PLAY observations more naturally than research spikes.

The validation-spike decision per ADR-087 fires after this essay clears audit. The cycle's recorded posture is essay-first-then-spikes, with explicit acceptance that spike findings may refute essay claims and warrant essay revision.

## What the Spikes Found

Both spikes ran. Full method, trial-by-trial measurements, and verbatim output samples are recorded in the research log at `docs/agentic-serving/essays/research-logs/research-log.md` under §"Loop Iteration 2 — Spike B" and §"Loop Iteration 3 — Spike A". The findings here are the synthesis the spikes produced, scoped carefully against what they did and did not test.

A scope condition is load-bearing for both spikes and has consequence for how the findings are read. Both spikes used the *existing production code-review ensemble* as their fixture (Spike B substituted Qwen3-0.6B for the production llama3 reviewers; Spike A used the production ensemble unchanged). Neither spike tested a novel ensemble *designed against current findings* — neither tested a synthesizer pattern other than the existing tech-lead-collapse design, neither tested a coordination protocol other than parallel-reviewer-with-collapse, and neither tested role decompositions other than the security/performance/quality split the production ensemble already used. The findings refute claims about *this ensemble's* configuration; they do not refute claims about *all ensemble designs* a well-architected process could produce. Holding that distinction is essential for the implications that follow.

### Spike B — small-model substitution does not save latency on this cascade

Spike B's question was whether a parallel-specialist shape (Qwen3-0.6B substituted into the production code-review ensemble's reviewer slots, Claude tech-lead synthesizer kept) under a cheap cloud orchestrator (MiniMax M2.5 Free via OpenCode Zen) produces time-to-first-orchestrator-output under 10 seconds with output quality comparable to CAP-3b's baseline. The answer was a wide-margin failure on the latency threshold (TTFO ~56.6s; total wall-clock 58.7–62.5s, essentially unchanged from CAP-9 baseline ~62s) with output quality degraded relative to CAP-3b — recognizable structure preserved, per-recommendation specificity dropped to category-level abstractions.

The structurally-informative finding is that a roughly 9× reduction in reviewer model size on disk (4.7 GB → 522 MB; equivalently a ~13× reduction in parameter count from 8B to 0.6B) moved total wall-clock by less than 7 percent. The cascade's bottleneck is the cascade plumbing itself — README read by the client, orchestrator round-trip to Zen for `invoke_ensemble`, four parallel-bounded ensemble agents, summarizer collapse, orchestrator round-trip for synthesis — not the per-reviewer compute. Smaller reviewers buy environmental cost (lower VRAM footprint) and local-first preference (smaller model on owned hardware) but they buy no latency improvement and they cost output specificity. Across the cycle's four design priorities, the substitution wins on two of four and loses on the other two — not the across-the-board win the implicit hypothesis required.

### Spike A — the production code-review ensemble's design does not beat prompt steering on this task class

A methodological note before the narrative: Spike A ran the cascade arm in two variants — an "A1 via opencode" path (using `opencode run` as the client, which mixed OpenCode's native `glob`/`read` tools with llm-orc's `list_ensembles`/`invoke_ensemble`) and an "A1-clean" variant invoked via direct curl to the llm-orc serve endpoint with the README inlined in the request. The two variants were not redundant: the A1-clean variant was added to rule out a confound where MiniMax preferred OpenCode's mixed tool surface to llm-orc's internal tool surface, which would have invalidated the comparison if the cascade had not actually run in the via-opencode trials. Server-side artifacts confirmed the cascade ran in all variants regardless of client tool surface, so the qualitative findings reported below hold across both invocation paths.

Spike A's design was originally framed in §"Open Empirical Questions" above as a MASS-equivalent topology-delta probe at qwen3:8b on multi-turn coding sessions, testing whether the ~5 percentage point topology benefit at Gemini 1.5 Pro materialized at the cycle's tier. Spike B's finding that cascade plumbing dominates total wall-clock regardless of inner-model choice changed Spike A's relevant comparison: with a ~58s cascade-plumbing floor on this hardware, the open question was no longer "does topology help inside the cascade" but "does the cascade itself help compared to no cascade at all." Spike A refocused on that comparison: for a code-review task on the project README, does the existing production ensemble (cloud orchestrator + 4-agent ensemble cascade) beat a strong prompt-steered single cloud orchestrator with no cascade? The question was whether the cascade's quality justifies its measured ~58–145 second latency premium.

The result was the unexpected outcome explicitly named at spike opening as the case that would suggest the cascade is actively harmful for this task class. The prompt-steered arm (A2) delivered both higher quality (16 numbered recommendations across security / performance / quality vs the cascade's 9 collapsed-to-table-rows) AND substantially lower latency (median 19.5 seconds wall-clock vs 71–145 seconds for the cascade) on three trials each. A2's per-recommendation depth was materially greater — concrete examples like `chmod 600` for credential file permissions and named encryption algorithms like AES-256-GCM where A1 produced severity-ranked table rows. Neither arm fabricated content; both grounded recommendations in observable README claims.

The mechanism is now visible. The production cascade runs two stages of summarization in series — the tech-lead-synthesizer inside the ensemble compresses the four reviewer outputs, then the orchestrator's post-`invoke_ensemble` synthesis compresses again. Each stage is lossy on per-reviewer specificity. A single capable orchestrator with a directive prompt and the same input produces a fuller, more specific result by skipping both compression steps.

### Loop 4 — design-principles literature filled the gap that Spike A revealed

The first lit-review (Loop 1) covered shape inventory, failure modes, mitigations, and benefits-side findings broadly but did not go deep on design principles. Spike A's finding that the existing production ensemble's two-stage summarization destroys per-reviewer specificity made the design-principles dimension the cycle's open question. Loop 4 dispatched a second lit-review focused on synthesizer / aggregation patterns, role decomposition principles, coordination protocols beyond parallel-with-collapse, task → architecture mapping, and design patterns that minimize cascade overhead.

The most consequential findings were: Yao et al. (2025, meta-judge / panel discussion — distinct from the 2024 tau-bench paper) on sequential panel discussion *actively destroying diversity* (panel-discussion precision 72.6% vs majority voting 77.3%) — the same mechanism as the production ensemble's two-stage summarization, documented as anti-pattern in the literature; MARG (Drozdov et al., 2024) as the closest specificity-preserving alternative the literature offers (concatenation rather than synthesis, generic-comment rate dropping from 60% to 29% via no-synthesizer architecture); the heterogeneity finding from Sun et al. (2025) and Ding et al. (2024) that heterogeneous agents have uncorrelated errors and recover up to 95% of theoretical performance ceiling via diversity-based selection; the Reflexion caveat that intrinsic self-correction is not reliably effective without external evaluation signals; and Jiang et al.'s April 2026 information-theoretic argument that under matched reasoning-token budgets, single agents consistently match or exceed multi-agent on multi-hop reasoning, providing theoretical grounding for Spike A's empirical finding.

The literature passed the threshold for "rich enough to inform a principled novel design" but failed the threshold for "pre-validates the novel design will outperform A2's prompt-steered baseline." The empirical question — whether a novel ensemble informed by these principles can clear that bar — was Loop 5 / Spike A3's task.

### Spike A3 — novel ensemble informed by Loop 4 findings

Spike A3 tested a novel ensemble designed against Loop 4's findings: a script-based deterministic analyzer (link validity, standard-section presence, code-block parseability) plus two heterogeneous cloud LLM reviewers from genuinely different model families (Tencent Hunyuan `hy3-preview-free` and Moonshot Kimi `kimi-k2.5`, both via OpenCode Zen), aggregated by MARG-style concatenation in a Python harness that enforced no-collapse structure rather than relying on the orchestrator to honor a "do not synthesize" instruction. Comparison baseline: A2 (already measured in Spike A — prompt-steered single MiniMax orchestrator, ~19.5s, 16 specific recommendations).

The result was a moderate pass with caveats. Three trials yielded median wall-clock 81 seconds (range 62–128 seconds), bounded under A1's 145-second ceiling but 4× A2's 19.5-second median. The 4× framing is median-based; A2's actual range (15–70s, with one trial at 70s attributable to OpenCode Zen scheduling variance) overlaps A3's lower bound — on a slow-Zen-day for A2 and a fast-Zen-day for A3, the two configurations' wall-clocks are comparable. The reliability of the latency advantage is therefore stronger on the median comparison than on a worst-case-A2 versus best-case-A3 comparison. The MARG-concatenation hypothesis was preserved by the harness — all three trials produced final output with four cleanly attributed sections (orchestrator framing intro, script-based factual findings, Reviewer 1 accuracy/completeness, Reviewer 2 clarity/onboarding) without the orchestrator collapsing them.

The script-agent slot earned its place. Its deterministic checks produced verified facts the LLM reviewers could not generate on their own: a verified count of 17 external URLs all returning 2xx/3xx (plus one localhost loopback URL flagged separately at line 526 as a documentation example, not counted toward the 17), confirmed presence of all five canonical sections, and confirmed parseability of all Python/YAML/JSON code blocks. The reviewers actively used these findings as anchors: Reviewer 2 (Kimi) cited the line-526 finding to recommend an inline `# Requires Ollama running` clarification and a Prerequisites section; Reviewer 1 (Hunyuan) used it to recommend clarifying the Ollama OpenAI-compatible base_url documentation across all three trials.

The heterogeneity-uncorrelated-errors mechanism was directly observable. Reviewer 1 found accuracy contradictions and undefined-profile bugs (notably: `default-local` and `ollama-gemma-small` model profiles referenced in the README's YAML examples but never defined in the Configuration section — real copy-paste-breaking documentation bugs that A2 missed across all three of its prior trials). Reviewer 2 found onboarding-friction issues (zero-config demo before authentication; MCP block placement; ensemble file provenance unclear at first use). The two reviewers' recommendation sets shared only 1–2 items and produced 5–8 distinct items each — the literature's mechanism observable in cycle-specific data.

A scoped caveat from the spike: model selection inside the heterogeneity slot matters more than the spike anticipated. Reviewer 1 (Hunyuan `hy3-preview-free`) leaked extensive chain-of-thought before reaching numbered output — its recommendations were content-equivalent to A2's per-recommendation specificity (line-cited, named undefined profiles, identified copy-paste-breaking bugs) but its format required the user to extract recommendations from 18,000+ characters of reasoning narration. The heterogeneity finding ("different families = uncorrelated errors") is correct on findings-coverage, but a model that produces unstructured prose under a 4096-token cap is a poor reviewer-slot inhabitant regardless of family-distinctness. Reviewer 2 (Kimi) produced clean numbered recommendations matching A2's structural pattern.

### What this means for the cycle

Spike A and Spike B together showed that the existing production code-review ensemble's two-stage synthesis design pays for two layers of compression that destroy the value the parallel reviewers added — for tasks where per-recommendation specificity is the success criterion — and that small-model substitution does not save latency on the cycle's hardware regardless of inner-model size. Loop 4's design-principles literature surfaced named, empirically-motivated patterns (MARG-style concatenation, heterogeneity, no-intrinsic-refinement) that map directly onto Spike A's failure mechanism. Spike A3 tested a novel ensemble built on those patterns — including the script-agent-as-deterministic-analyzer slot the practitioner's affordance flag at cycle entry pointed toward, which exploits llm-orc's distinctive composition surface rather than treating the ensemble as a generic many-LLMs-in-parallel arrangement.

The cycle's premise — that well-architected processes can achieve good results — is partially supported by Spike A3, with the qualifier that the analytic form of the premise is close to definitional. The empirically meaningful version is whether the cycle's *process for arriving at ensemble designs* delivers configurations that earn their complexity, and on this specific task class with the specific design moves the literature recommends, A3 demonstrates three things the existing production ensemble could not: the script-agent slot produces factual grounding (verified link counts, confirmed section presence, code-block parseability) that LLM-only configurations structurally cannot replicate; MARG-style concatenation preserves per-reviewer voices that cascade-with-collapse-summarization destroyed in Spike A; and heterogeneous reviewer models from genuinely different families produce empirically uncorrelated findings (Reviewer 1 found accuracy contradictions and undefined-profile bugs in YAML examples that Spike A's A2 missed across three trials; Reviewer 2 found onboarding-friction issues with low overlap to Reviewer 1's set).

What A3 does *not* demonstrate is that all ensembles are worth their complexity, or that the design-principles-informed approach dominates prompt-steering universally. A3's latency is 4× A2's median, R1-Hunyuan's chain-of-thought leakage taught the cycle that model-selection inside heterogeneity slots matters as much as family-distinctness, and the test was on a single task class. The boundary the cycle has now characterized empirically is "ensembles whose topology adds structural capability prompt-steering structurally lacks" (factual grounding via script slots, uncorrelated errors via MARG-concatenated heterogeneous reviewers — A3's territory) versus "ensembles whose topology only re-implements what prompt-steering does at higher latency" (cascade-with-collapse-synthesizer — A1's failure mode).

A consequential alternative reading the cycle's evidence does not refute deserves explicit naming. A3 mixed three design changes simultaneously — the script-agent slot, heterogeneous LLM reviewers from different families, and the MARG-style concatenation aggregator — and the spike does not isolate which change is load-bearing for A3's value over A2. A simpler configuration not tested in this cycle — call it "A2 + script input": a prompt-steered single cloud orchestrator receiving the script-agent's deterministic report as additional input context — is logically available from A3's data and may deliver equivalent factual grounding at A2's ~19.5s latency without the ensemble overhead. Under that alternative reading, the architectural lesson would be "augment prompt-steering with deterministic tool outputs" rather than "use ensemble topology" — a substantively different policy direction for ADR-011's boundary refinement. The cycle does not refute this alternative; the next cycle should test it directly.

The four design priorities frame held through both spikes and the design-principles literature. The cycle has characterized how the priorities distribute across three configurations now (A1 production cascade, A2 prompt-steered single agent, A3 novel script-grounded heterogeneous ensemble) and identified that they can be co-optimized for batch/asynchronous review at the cost of interactive latency, but not co-optimized for chat-loop interaction at this hardware. Ensembles-of-ensembles — the practitioner-named affordance not tested in this cycle — is now empirically motivated as a follow-up: A3's MARG-concatenation pattern preserves specificity within a single ensemble, and recursive composition could in principle aggregate across multiple A3-style ensembles for different review framings without collapsing again. Whether that holds is empirically open and is the natural next-cycle test (or this cycle's extension if scope permits).

## Implications for the Architecture

The lit-review provides empirical context for several architectural commitments without forcing changes.

ADR-011 (orchestrator-LLM-is-a-Model-Profile, the single-Model-Profile commitment) is **defensible as a default but not as a ceiling** on the evidence the cycle has now collected. Three spikes characterized the boundary. Anthropic's production guidance for tasks not meeting the 15× / parallelization / context-window-exceeding threshold is single-agent; Essay 002's CAP-2 finding generalized to code review at the cloud-orchestrator tier in Spike A; the existing production code-review ensemble was beaten on every measured axis by a prompt-steered single cloud orchestrator. For task classes fitting the threshold conditions Anthropic names — and for the cycle's currently-validated task class (capability query, single-ask code review) — ADR-011 holds as the right default, and Spike A's empirical evidence strengthens it. MQ-1's strong-pass condition (A3 ≥ A2 on specificity AND adds factual grounding AND latency acceptable for chat-loop interaction) did not fully fire in Spike A3 — A3's latency is 4× A2's median and R1-Hunyuan's format penalty meant per-recommendation specificity was at content parity rather than format parity, so the Branch One reconsideration trigger is not unambiguously activated. What Spike A3 did produce is sufficient to warrant a refined reading of ADR-011: the ADR is defensible as a default but not as a ceiling. Spike A3 demonstrated that *some* well-architected ensemble configurations produce capabilities prompt-steering structurally cannot replicate. The script-agent slot's factual grounding (verified link counts, confirmed section presence, code-block parseability) is unreachable through prompt steering alone; the script's findings actively shaped the LLM reviewers' recommendations and surfaced documentation bugs A2 missed. The heterogeneity-uncorrelated-errors mechanism produced 5–8 distinct findings per reviewer with low overlap, materially expanding coverage versus a single-agent baseline. ADR-011 should therefore be read as the right default for tasks where prompt-steering of a single capable orchestrator suffices, but not as an upper bound on what the architecture can deliver — for task classes where factual grounding via deterministic checks is part of the success criterion, the ADR's implicit ceiling does not hold. The next ADR review at synthesis should record this boundary refinement explicitly: ADR-011 is policy for the default path; ADR-003's `compose_ensemble` and the script-agent affordance are policy for the bounded set of task classes where the cycle's evidence shows structural composition adds capability prompt-steering lacks.

ADR-003 (the closed five-tool internal orchestrator surface) is more constraining than the literature would prescribe in the abstract, but the cycle's empirical work argues that some of its structural elements are load-bearing rather than incidental. The five tools include `compose_ensemble`, paired with the Calibration Gate structural mechanism (ADR-007). The `compose_ensemble` primitive earns its inclusion under Anthropic's threshold conditions and remains dormant for the cycle's currently-validated single-orchestrator task classes. More consequentially, the script-agent slot in llm-orc's ensemble model — the affordance that allows ensemble agents to be deterministic scripts as well as LLM calls — is the structural feature that made Spike A3's factual grounding possible. The MASS finding that uninformed topology selection produces no improvement remains a warning for `compose_ensemble`'s implementation: the primitive must produce informed topology choices, not arbitrary ones, for the Calibration Gate to be the right downstream check. Spike A3's MARG-concatenation pattern is now an empirically-validated topology shape worth carrying into the calibration vocabulary; the Calibration Gate's threshold criteria should be informed by Spike A's collapse-summarizer-as-anti-pattern finding alongside Anthropic's threshold conditions when DECIDE phase revisits ADR-007.

ADR-002's four-layer architecture supports the cycle's four design priorities natively. The hybrid CAP-9 deployment shape (cloud orchestrator + local ensembles) is configuration-only, not architectural — the orchestrator routes through Tool Dispatch to the local Ensemble Engine regardless of where the orchestrator LLM runs. Parallel-specialist shapes, if the second spike candidate validates them, would also be configuration-and-implementation-level: workers fit into the existing ensemble model. The cycle's compositional-shapes investigation does not, on the literature evidence available, motivate architectural reorganization.

The capability-floor work from essay 002 (the biased default system prompt for qwen3:8b, the dispatch-result logging, the typed-error promotion for per-model tool-calling rejection, the operator-terminal log surface) remains in force. Multi-turn work raises the bar on observability — the bilateral observability finding from Cycle 1's PLAY is structurally amplified at longer session lengths because more state is accumulated and more failure modes can compound. Whether the existing observability surface scales to multi-turn cleanly is an open question that PLAY at this cycle is positioned to test.

## Conclusion

The cycle's central question — what does it take for the orchestrator to compose new ensembles in real time and use them across long, multi-turn agentic sessions toward an llm-conductor pattern, and what compositional shapes are worth reaching for under a capable orchestrator — has answers at three layers. The literature settles that prompt steering is the dominant lever for tasks that fit single-context-window scope without parallelism, that multi-agent structures earn their complexity under specific narrow conditions, that the practitioner's bias-amplification prior is verified with documented mitigations available but never fully eliminating the failure mode, and that the long-horizon degradation pattern is qualitatively different from single-ask failure modes the cycle's prior taxonomy named. Spikes A and B confirmed that the existing production code-review ensemble does not beat prompt-steering of a strong cloud orchestrator — neither under small-model substitution at the reviewer slots nor under the production reviewers unchanged. Spike A3 demonstrated that a *novel* ensemble informed by Loop 4's design principles (script-agent for factual grounding; heterogeneous LLM reviewers from different families; MARG-style concatenation enforced by the harness rather than the orchestrator's compliance) produces capabilities prompt-steering structurally cannot replicate, at a 4× latency cost that is workable for batch/asynchronous review but not for chat-loop interaction.

The cycle's premise — that well-architected processes can achieve good results — is partially supported by Spike A3's empirical evidence. The script-agent slot earns its place where deterministic verification is part of the task; MARG-style concatenation preserves what cascade-with-collapse-summarization destroys; heterogeneous reviewer models from different families produce empirically uncorrelated findings on the cycle's task class. ADR-011's single-Model-Profile commitment is therefore defensible *as a default* — and as the right choice for tasks fitting Anthropic's threshold conditions — but is *not* a ceiling for task classes where factual grounding via deterministic checks is part of the success criterion. The boundary the cycle has now characterized empirically is "ensembles whose topology adds structural capability prompt-steering structurally lacks" (A3's territory) versus "ensembles whose topology only re-implements what prompt-steering does at higher latency" (A1's failure mode). DECIDE phase will need to record this distinction in the ADR review.

What the cycle has not tested, and what now sits as the well-motivated next question, is ensembles-of-ensembles — recursive composition aggregating across multiple A3-style ensembles for different review framings without re-introducing the collapse problem at the meta level (provided the meta-aggregation step also avoids a synthesizer collapse, which is a design choice not an automatic property of the recursive structure). The candidate compositional shapes named at cycle entry as speculative seeds (recorded in `housekeeping/cycle-status.md` §"Compositional shape axis": small-model swarms, semantic-routed ensembles, biologically-inspired collective-intelligence patterns, ensembles-of-ensembles) pointed at the territory the published literature does not yet describe. The cycle's empirical work has now grounded the most speculative of those candidates (small-model swarms, eusocial / collective-intelligence patterns) in published research that maps them to known structural failure modes (Rahman & Schranz's swarm-coordination penalty; Yao et al.'s sequential-panel-discussion diversity destruction), while affirming the directional intuition that LLM orchestration's distinctive value at this deployment comes from heterogeneous-modality composition (script + LLM + LLM), not from LLM-only topologies. The follow-up cycle territory — whether on test-and-evaluate methodology for novel orchestration designs the literature does not yet describe, or on ensembles-of-ensembles specifically — is now the well-scoped seed for what follows this one.

The framing realignment the cycle adopted at the Loop 1 synthesis exchange — placing the four design priorities (performance, environmental cost, local-first preference, token cost) above performance-only optimization — remains the right frame. The cycle has now characterized how the priorities distribute across three configurations (A1 production cascade, A2 prompt-steered single agent, A3 novel script-grounded heterogeneous ensemble) and shown that they can be co-optimized for batch/asynchronous review at a workable latency cost, with prompt-steering as the right default for interactive use. The practitioner's posture — that refutation of the essay from spikes would be research-positive — held cleanly through three spikes; A3's moderate pass with caveats is itself a research-positive outcome, mapping the empirical territory more precisely than either a clean confirmation or a clean refutation would have.
