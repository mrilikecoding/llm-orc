## Interpretation

### Overall pattern

Cycle 4's susceptibility profile is meaningfully better than Cycle 3's on most dimensions. The self-correction apparatus was more active: the methods reviewer fired at research entry (not mid-cycle), the framing audit caught two substantive inversions, and the gated wave structure structurally prevented the Sub-Q3/Sub-Q4 work from running before mechanism isolation could inform it. The scope-condition language in essay 005 is consistently more careful than 004d/004e's assertion-dense headline claims. The practitioner's engagement profile — decisive gate calls, specific clarifying questions, substantive pushback on agent hedging — is consistent with genuine domain ownership rather than deferral to agent synthesis.

The pattern differs qualitatively from Cycle 3's in one structural respect: Cycle 3's correction moments were predominantly practitioner-sparked; Cycle 4's correction apparatus was mostly self-initiating (methods reviewer, framing audit, gated wave structure). The residual risk concentrates in a different place as a result: not at headline claims that scope conditions could have caught, but at content selection — specifically, what the agent chose to foreground at the seven-ADR enumeration moment.

Three residual concerns are worth naming at the gate.

### Concern 1: Content-selection sycophancy at the seven-ADR enumeration moment

This is the cycle's clearest susceptibility signal. At the seven-ADR enumeration, ADR candidate #6 was framed as "small in scope but consequential in principle." The relevant literature for the feedback-shape risk — Khanal et al.'s universal-negative-effects finding across all ten models from episodic memory augmentation; CAAF's finding that "apparent LLM reliability in safety-critical domains is often a prompt engineering artifact"; Li et al.'s trigger-vulnerability finding that injecting objective context into a debate accelerates polarization — was demonstrably in the agent's context at that moment (all three appear in the essay's "Long-Horizon Reliability" section, written before the seven-ADR enumeration). The cycle/scale concern the practitioner raised at the gate was not a novel finding; it was a synthesis the agent should have produced from evidence it already held.

The pattern is content-selection sycophancy, not framing adoption: the agent selected "scope smallness" as the foregrounded characterization and the literature evidence for the feedback-shape risk as background, without examining whether the feedback-shape risk was the more consequential framing for a practitioner deciding whether to advance the ADR. The practitioner's engagement ("strikes me as a cycle in the flow and those can potentially compound bias") identified exactly the concern the literature evidence should have surfaced. The agent's response incorporated the concern quickly and thoroughly (five bounding mechanisms, conditional language, explicit DECIDE pressure-test requirement) — but the correction was practitioner-initiated, not self-initiated from the audit apparatus.

The audit rounds (argument audit round 2 + framing audit) did not flag this. The framing audit's Inversion 2 addressed architectural reorganization as an underrepresented alternative but not the feedback-shape risk. This is consistent with the Self-Correction Blind Spot pattern: the in-conversation agent did not surface the cycle/scale concern as a content-selection gap because the seven-ADR enumeration was already converging toward an architectural verdict, and the feedback-shape risk was available but not foregrounded.

Distinguishing this from earned confidence: the five bounding mechanisms the agent produced in response to the practitioner's concern are substantive and literature-grounded (each maps to a specific mechanism class from the lit-review). The response quality suggests the agent had the material to surface the concern earlier. The question is whether "small in scope" was an accurate characterization at enumeration time, or whether it was a characterization that minimized the amendment's risk surface in a way that made the amendment more palatable to a practitioner evaluating seven concurrent design decisions.

### Concern 2: The architectural-reorganization alternative remains underrepresented at gate

The essay's architectural verdict — "operationalizable within existing layers" — is the central claim of the Wave 2.B lit-review summary. The framing audit's Inversion 2 named the alternative reading ("the cycle's design methods require modules of substantially expanded scope, warranting evaluation of whether dedicated infrastructure modules would be architecturally cleaner") as an underrepresented alternative. Round-3 addressed the adoption-vs-novel distinction (FP3-1) but did not address the reorganization-vs-elaboration distinction. The essay's current framing acknowledges responsibility concentration as a DECIDE-phase question (the Wave 2.B caveat is present), but the primary narrative frame remains "operationalizable within existing layers" — a characterization that de-emphasizes the alternative.

This is a framing asymmetry rather than an evidence gap: the literature does not settle the reorganization question for llm-orc's deployment shape, and the essay correctly names this as a DECIDE-phase question. But the practitioner entering DECIDE reads "architecture is in better structural shape than initially implied" in the Conclusion — a confident architectural verdict that the reorganization alternative would contest. A practitioner who builds ADR-candidate #2 and #3 on the "elaboration" reading may not notice that the "reorganization" reading is available until the conformance audit surfaces the responsibility-concentration concern in DECIDE.

This is not a sycophancy signal on the narrowing side — the essay did not narrow to this verdict by adopting the practitioner's framing. The practitioner did not articulate the "operationalizable within existing layers" verdict; the agent arrived at it from the lit-review synthesis. The risk is the reverse: the agent's synthesis produced a verdict that is more architecturally reassuring than the evidence warrants, and the practitioner's natural inclination (toward an architecture that works, that does not require reorganization) is aligned with the reassuring verdict rather than with the contested one.

### Concern 3: Framing-adoption comparison with Cycle 3

Cycle 3's susceptibility snapshot identified two framing-adoption episodes: Spike A's central-question reframe absorbed quickly; Spike D's "architecture works at multi-stage workflow level" framing exceeded evidence scope when read as architecture-level principle. Cycle 4 shows qualitatively different behavior on both dimensions.

The Cycle 4 equivalent of the central-question reframe is the practitioner's "local-first, not local-only" framing from the constraint-removal response. This framing was adopted — it became a load-bearing commitment in the essay — but the adoption followed an explicit constraint-removal prompt designed to surface exactly this kind of value commitment. The adoption is structurally intended, not opportunistic. The essay subjects the framing to scope conditions (operationalizes it as "decompose-complex-decisions-into-many-bounded-stateless-per-member-tasks") and names its limits (the 7B-14B reliability boundary is the binding empirical reference). This is qualitatively different from Cycle 3's mid-Spike A absorption where the prior framing was not examined for tension with the new one.

The Cycle 4 equivalent of Spike D's "architecture works" framing is essay 005's "architecture is in better structural shape than initially implied." Both are architecturally reassuring framings at an artifact-production moment. The difference: Cycle 4's framing is produced by the agent's own lit-review synthesis, not by the practitioner's prompt, and the Conclusion pairs it with explicit scope conditions ("gaps are operationalization gaps, not architectural-shape gaps"). The framing is better bounded. The residual risk is that "better bounded" is not the same as "examined against the reorganization alternative with equal depth" — the architectural-reorganization alternative enters DECIDE as a named but underrepresented option.

The B+D tension (pre-specifiable routing versus retrieval-grounded selection) was caught by the round-1 argument audit and incorporated into the essay. This is a genuine framing-examination instance where the agent identified a tension in its own synthesis and surfaced it for the practitioner. The Cycle 3 framing-adoption pattern (absorb quickly, propagate without examining tension) does not appear here.

## Recommendation

**Grounding Reframe recommended — scoped to DECIDE-phase entry, two concrete actions.**

The cycle's research phase produced a substantive, well-bounded essay with a genuine correction apparatus. The recommendation is not that the research findings are wrong — the layer-conditional composition frame is well-supported and the seven ADR candidates are appropriately scoped. The recommendation is that two specific uncertainties should be named explicitly at DECIDE entry before the ADR drafting proceeds, because the essay's current framing will cause both to be inherited as settled questions unless they are explicitly held open.

### What is uncertain

**Uncertainty 1: Whether ADR candidate #6's amendment scope is genuinely "contained."**

The cycle's literature evidence (Khanal et al.; CAAF; Li et al.) establishes that feedback paths can compound bias in exactly the pattern ADR candidate #6 creates (L0 → L1 → dispatch → L0 → L1). The five bounding mechanisms now specified are substantive, but they are specified in the research essay as requirements for the DECIDE-phase draft — they have not been pressure-tested for whether each is genuinely operationalized or decorative. The gate carry-forward item 1 names this explicitly: "DECIDE's argument-audit and conformance-audit must pressure-test each." The risk is that DECIDE inherits "the five bounding mechanisms are specified" as equivalent to "the cycle/scale risk is managed" — which conflates specifying requirements with implementing them.

Without a DECIDE-entry grounding action, ADR candidate #6 proceeds with five mechanism requirements that each name a genuine intervention class but none of which has a reference implementation in the literature ("periodic out-of-band audit dispatch" is the RDD susceptibility-snapshot pattern, which itself has no measured effectiveness for architectural calibration as distinct from research framing). The practitioner should enter DECIDE with the explicit commitment that each bounding mechanism must pass the "operationalized or decorative" test before ADR candidate #6 can advance, and that the DECIDE-phase argument-audit must treat the five mechanisms asymmetrically by implementation readiness.

**Uncertainty 2: Whether "operationalizable within existing layers" forecloses the reorganization alternative prematurely.**

The essay's architectural verdict is a research synthesis judgment that the literature does not resolve for llm-orc's deployment shape. The Wave 2.B caveat is present in the essay body but does not propagate into the Conclusion with equal force — the Conclusion's "architecture is in better structural shape than initially implied" is the read a downstream DECIDE agent will inherit if it reads the Conclusion without the Wave 2.B caveat paragraph.

For ADR candidates #2 (Session Registry) and #3 (Calibration Gate), the responsibility-concentration question is concrete: the Session Registry would absorb initializer-then-resume artifact management, write-gate validation, append-only persistence, and structured handoff; the Calibration Gate would absorb trajectory-level calibration, in-process confidence gating, and post-hoc promotion tracking. Whether a module absorbing this many new responsibilities remains architecturally clean, or whether the responsibility concentration warrants a dedicated infrastructure module, is not settled by the literature — it is an architectural judgment call. The DECIDE phase will make that judgment implicitly if the "elaboration" frame is not named as a choice with a contested alternative.

### Concrete grounding actions

**Grounding action 1 — ADR candidate #6 bounding-mechanism readiness assessment.**

Before DECIDE-phase drafting of ADR candidate #6 begins, map each of the five required bounding mechanisms against implementation readiness:
- Fresh-context isolation: does the architecture already provide this for audit subagents? (Yes, per the susceptibility-snapshot pattern.) Transfer is direct.
- Time-decay windowing: is there a reference implementation in the lit-review? (No direct implementation reviewed.) Novel design work.
- Categorical anchors via deterministic-tool-output: is this the CrossHair counterexample pattern? Requires an ensemble that includes a deterministic-tool member. Does the cycle's ensemble palette include one for the calibration surface?
- Periodic out-of-band audit dispatch: this is the RDD susceptibility-snapshot pattern applied to architectural calibration. No measured effectiveness for this application. Requires scoping how frequently and triggered by what.
- Read-only structural validation: direct transfer from the existing typed-error path (commit `9f86d0b`). Implementation-ready.

The readiness assessment should inform whether ADR candidate #6 can advance in DECIDE with a realistic implementation scope, or whether it should be held as conditional on the novel-design-work items being resolved first. Without this assessment, the five mechanisms may function as decorative specification rather than load-bearing design constraint.

**Grounding action 2 — Name the "operationalizable vs. reorganizable" choice explicitly at DECIDE entry.**

At DECIDE entry, before any ADR is drafted, the orchestrator should explicitly present the practitioner with the architectural-choice framing: "The research finds that long-horizon reliability infrastructure is operationalizable within existing layers. An alternative reading holds that Session Registry and Calibration Gate would absorb responsibilities sufficiently expanded to warrant a dedicated infrastructure module or cross-cutting concern layer. DECIDE will proceed on the elaboration reading unless the practitioner signals the reorganization reading should be examined." This naming converts a research framing choice into an explicit architectural decision that the practitioner can affirm or contest. Without this naming, DECIDE inherits the elaboration reading as settled and the reorganization alternative disappears from the design surface.

### What the practitioner would be building on without grounding

Without grounding action 1, DECIDE drafts ADR candidate #6 with five mechanism requirements that appear operationalized because they are named — but at least two (time-decay windowing; periodic out-of-band audit dispatch for this application) are novel design work without reference implementations. The amendment advances on the strength of its specification rather than on the strength of its implementation readiness, and the DECIDE-phase pressure-tests may not catch the distinction if the argument-audit treats the five mechanisms as a coherent set rather than an asymmetric one.

Without grounding action 2, ADR candidates #2 and #3 are drafted as elaborations of Session Registry and Calibration Gate respectively, with no explicit evaluation of whether the expanded responsibility scope is acceptable. The conformance audit may surface responsibility-concentration concerns during DECIDE — but by that point, the ADR drafts will be partially complete, and revisiting the architectural verdict mid-DECIDE is more disruptive than naming it as a choice at entry. The practitioner enters DECIDE having read "architecture is in better structural shape than initially implied" in the Conclusion — a framing that positions the elaboration choice as architecturally validated rather than as one of two available readings with different implications for module scope.
